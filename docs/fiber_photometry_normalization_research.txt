1. IS BASELINE-REFERENCED Z-SCORING THE STANDARD?
================================================================================

SHORT ANSWER: Yes, baseline-referenced z-scoring is a widely used standard, but
the field has NO single consensus method. The choice of baseline period is critical
and can introduce artifacts if done improperly.

DETAILED FINDINGS:

1a. Z-scoring is the most common normalization for cross-subject comparisons
---------------------------------------------------------------------
The standard formula is:
    z = (dF/F - mean_baseline) / std_baseline

This transforms the signal into units of standard deviation relative to a baseline
period, enabling comparison across animals with different absolute fluorescence
levels, fiber placements, and sensor expression.

Simpson et al. (2024, Neuron) -- "Lights, fiber, action! A primer on in vivo
fiber photometry" -- explicitly notes that z-scoring is the most common approach
for normalizing photometry data for cross-subject comparison, but cautions that
there is no consensus on best practices. They describe a standard preprocessing
pipeline: (1) isosbestic correction, (2) dF/F calculation, (3) z-scoring for
group comparisons.

Sherathiya et al. (2021, Scientific Reports) -- GuPPy toolbox paper from the
Lerner lab -- implements z-scoring as a core step: after computing dF/F using
isosbestic (405nm) control channel regression, the signal is z-scored for
peri-event analysis. The z-score baseline is typically a pre-event window.

1b. Types of baseline periods used in the literature
---------------------------------------------------------------------
| Baseline Type              | Typical Window        | Use Case                    |
|----------------------------|-----------------------|-----------------------------|
| Pre-event (trial-level)    | -5 to -1 sec          | Discrete stimuli/events     |
| Session-wide               | Entire recording      | Continuous state changes    |
| Designated pre-task period  | Recording start to    | Chandni's approach          |
|                            | manipulation onset    |                             |
| Sliding window             | Moving 30-120 sec     | Long recordings with drift  |

1c. Concerns about baseline period selection
---------------------------------------------------------------------
- BASELINE STABILITY: If the baseline period itself is noisy or contains
  behavioral artifacts, the resulting z-scores will be distorted. A noisy baseline
  inflates sigma, compressing all z-scores. A quiet baseline deflates sigma,
  inflating z-scores.

- BASELINE DURATION: Must be long enough for stable mean/std estimates but short
  enough to avoid contamination from preceding events. 2-5 seconds is common for
  trial-level; for session-level baselines (like Chandni's approach), the entire
  pre-manipulation period can be used.

- ANTICIPATORY ACTIVITY: Many researchers leave a 0.5-1 sec buffer before event
  onset to exclude any anticipatory neural activity from the baseline calculation.

- STATIONARITY ASSUMPTION: Z-scoring assumes the baseline period is stationary
  (constant mean and variance). If the baseline drifts (e.g., due to incomplete
  bleach correction), the z-score reference is invalid.

CHANDNI'S APPROACH (recording start to heating onset as baseline) is reasonable
and is used in many published studies where a pre-manipulation baseline exists.
The key requirement is that this period should be long enough (ideally >2 minutes
after initial photobleaching stabilizes) and the animal should be in a comparable
behavioral state during baseline as during the experimental period.

2. Z-SCORED SIGNAL FOR MEAN ANALYSES vs. RAW dF/F FOR TRANSIENT PROPERTIES
================================================================================

SHORT ANSWER: This is scientifically well-justified and directly supported by
Wallace et al. (2025). Z-scoring distorts transient kinetics; raw dF/F preserves
them.

2a. What Wallace et al. (2025) specifically says
---------------------------------------------------------------------
CITATION: Wallace CW, Slinkard CY, Shaughnessy R, Holleran KM, Centanni SW,
Lapish CC, Jones SR. "Fiber photometry analysis of spontaneous dopamine signals:
The z-scored data are not the data." bioRxiv (2025); published ACS Chemical
Neuroscience (2025). DOI: 10.1101/2025.02.19.639080

KEY FINDINGS:
- Wallace et al. compared three methods for analyzing spontaneous dLight dopamine
  transients: (1) Z-Score Method, (2) Manual Method (raw %dF/F with manual
  baseline adjustment), and (3) Prominence Method (z-score for peak detection,
  then return to raw data for kinetics).

- THE Z-SCORE METHOD FAILED TO DETECT:
  * SCH23390-induced reduction in signal frequency (z-scoring amplified noise
    when true signals were diminished, creating false "events" from noise)
  * Cocaine-induced increases in signal width (z-scoring normalizes away the
    absolute width differences)
  * Raclopride-induced increases in amplitude (z-scoring destroys absolute
    amplitude information)

- THE MANUAL METHOD (using raw %dF/F) correctly detected ALL pharmacological
  changes in frequency, amplitude, and width.

- THE PROMINENCE METHOD worked as a compromise: use z-scoring to identify/tag
  peaks, then return to the preprocessed (non-z-scored) dF/F data to measure
  peak kinetic properties (amplitude, width).

- THEIR CONCLUSION (quoted from abstract): "Analysis of spontaneous dopamine
  signals requires assessment of the %dF/F values, ideally using the Manual
  Method, and the use of z-scoring is not appropriate."

2b. Scientific rationale for the distinction
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Z-SCORING IS APPROPRIATE FOR:
- Mean signal level comparisons (e.g., "does average fluorescence increase
  during heating?")
- Peri-event time histograms (PETH) averaged across trials/animals
- Comparing relative signal changes across animals with different SNR
- Any analysis where you want to compare the RELATIVE magnitude of a response
  in units of baseline variability

Z-SCORING IS NOT APPROPRIATE FOR:
- Transient amplitude measurements (z-scoring normalizes by std, so an animal
  with noisier baseline will have smaller z-scored amplitudes for the same true
  signal)
- Transient half-width / duration measurements (z-scoring can shift the apparent
  crossing points of threshold-based width measurements)
- Transient frequency analysis (when signal is reduced, z-scoring amplifies
  noise to fill the "void," creating spurious events)
- Any kinetic property that depends on absolute signal magnitude

WHY: Z-scoring is a linear transformation z = (x - mu) / sigma. This:
  (1) Shifts the baseline to zero (good for mean comparisons)
  (2) Scales by noise level (good for cross-animal comparison)
  BUT (2) also means that transient amplitudes in dF/F units are lost. An animal
  with 2% dF/F transients and 0.5% noise gets z-amplitudes of ~4, while an animal
  with 2% dF/F transients and 1% noise gets z-amplitudes of ~2 -- same biology,
  different z-scores. For transient analysis, you want the actual 2% dF/F value.

2c. Chandni's approach is well-justified
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Using z-dF/F for mean signal analyses and raw dF/F for transient properties
(amplitude, half-width) is consistent with:
- Wallace et al. (2025) recommendations
- The "Prominence Method" approach (z-score for detection, raw for kinetics)
- General signal processing principles

3. ALTERNATIVE NORMALIZATION METHODS
================================================================================

3a. Robust z-score using MAD (Median Absolute Deviation)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
FORMULA: robust_z = (x - median_baseline) / (1.4826 * MAD_baseline)
WHERE: MAD = median(|x - median(x)|)
The factor 1.4826 makes MAD consistent with std for normal distributions.

ADVANTAGES:
- Resistant to outliers (large transients in baseline don't inflate sigma)
- Better for baselines with occasional spikes or artifacts
- Recommended by some toolboxes when baseline contains neural events

WHEN TO USE: When baseline period may contain transient neural activity or
motion artifacts that would skew the standard deviation estimate.

REFERENCE: This approach is discussed in the alphaXiv research survey and
implemented in several analysis pipelines.

3b. Percentile normalization
---------------------------------------------------------------------
FORMULA: normalized = (x - F_low) / (F_high - F_low)
WHERE: F_low = e.g., 10th percentile, F_high = e.g., 90th percentile of the
signal (or of a baseline period).

ADVANTAGES:
- Robust to outliers
- Bounds the signal between 0 and 1 (approximately)
- Does not assume normality

WHEN TO USE: For very long recordings or when signal distribution is non-Gaussian.

3c. dF/F0 with sliding baseline (running baseline)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
FORMULA: dF/F = (F - F0) / F0
WHERE: F0 = running median or percentile (e.g., 10th percentile in a sliding
window of 30-120 seconds).

ADVANTAGES:
- Adapts to slow drifts (incomplete bleach correction, slow state changes)
- Does not require a single fixed baseline period
- Used by Martianova et al. (2019) in their processing pipeline

DISADVANTAGES:
- Can subtract out slow, real biological signals (e.g., sustained activity
  changes during heating)
- Window size selection is somewhat arbitrary
- CRITICAL FOR CHANDNI'S EXPERIMENT: A sliding baseline during the heating
  period would SUBTRACT OUT the very signal you're trying to measure (the
  sustained change during hyperthermia). This approach is NOT recommended for
  the heating experiment unless you want to look only at fast transients.

3d. Isosbestic regression-based dF/F (the standard preprocessing step)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
FORMULA: dF/F = (F_signal - F_fitted_control) / F_fitted_control
WHERE: F_fitted_control = isosbestic (405nm) signal fitted to the 465nm signal
via regression.

This is the PREPROCESSING step, not an alternative to z-scoring. It should be
done BEFORE z-scoring. The fitted control captures photobleaching and motion
artifacts.

KEY ADVANCE: Keevers & Jean-Richard-dit-Bressel (2025, Neurophotonics) showed
that robust regression (IRLS - iteratively reweighted least squares) is SUPERIOR
to ordinary least squares (OLS) for fitting the isosbestic to the signal channel.
OLS regression can be biased by neural events in the signal channel, causing the
fit to "chase" real transients and subtract them out. IRLS downweights these
outlier points.

RECOMMENDATION: Use IRLS regression for isosbestic correction, then compute dF/F,
then z-score if needed for group comparisons.

3e. Martianova (2019) zdFF method
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Martianova et al. developed a specific pipeline (available on GitHub:
katemartian/Photometry_data_processing) that computes a "zdFF" measure:
- Smooth both 405nm and 465nm signals
- Use airPLS (adaptive iteratively reweighted penalized least squares) for
  baseline estimation
- Compute standardized dF/F

This is implemented in their get_zdFF.py function and has been widely adopted.

4. TEMPERATURE SENSITIVITY OF GCaMP IN HYPERTHERMIA EXPERIMENTS
================================================================================

THIS IS THE MOST CRITICAL ISSUE FOR CHANDNI'S EXPERIMENT.

4a. GFP/GCaMP fluorescence is inherently temperature-sensitive
---------------------------------------------------------------------
GFP-based fluorescent proteins (including GCaMP) show DECREASED fluorescence
with INCREASED temperature. This is a fundamental photophysical property:

- GFP fluorescence decreases approximately 1-2% per degree Celsius in the
  physiological range (25-42C). This is well-documented in the biophysics
  literature.

  REFERENCES:
  * Nieder et al. (2019, Scientific Reports): "GFP fluorescence peak fraction
    analysis based nanothermometer" -- demonstrated ~0.26% per degree C
    sensitivity for wtGFP and ~2.5% per degree C for emGFP-Mito in cells.
  * Zhang et al. (2009, Applied Microbiology and Biotechnology): GFPmut1
    fluorescence is "thermally sensitive" -- fluorescence decreases with
    increasing temperature in the physiological range.
  * dos Santos (2011, Journal of Fluorescence): Studied GFP fluorescence from
    20-75C, showing progressive quenching with temperature increase.
  * Leiderman et al. (2005, Biophysical Journal): "Transition in the
    Temperature-Dependence of GFP Fluorescence" -- detailed mechanism of
    temperature-dependent quenching via proton dynamics.

- The mechanism: increased temperature accelerates non-radiative decay pathways
  (thermal quenching), reduces the quantum yield, and shifts the protonation
  equilibrium of the chromophore.

4b. Implications for hyperthermia fiber photometry experiments
---------------------------------------------------------------------
In a hyperthermia experiment where brain temperature increases:

PROBLEM 1 -- DIRECT THERMAL QUENCHING:
GCaMP fluorescence will DECREASE due to thermal quenching alone, independent of
any change in neural activity or calcium. If brain temperature rises by 3-5
degrees C (a typical hyperthermia range), you could see a 3-10% decrease in
baseline fluorescence purely from temperature effects.

PROBLEM 2 -- CALCIUM-INDEPENDENT FLUORESCENCE CHANGE:
GCaMP's calcium-free (apo) state fluorescence and calcium-bound (sat) state
fluorescence are BOTH affected by temperature, but potentially to different
degrees. This means the dynamic range (F_max/F_min) of the sensor may change
with temperature.

PROBLEM 3 -- THE ISOSBESTIC CHANNEL IS ALSO AFFECTED:
The 405nm (isosbestic) channel fluorescence will ALSO decrease with temperature
(it's still exciting GCaMP, just at the calcium-insensitive wavelength). This
means the isosbestic correction SHOULD partially compensate for the thermal
artifact, since both channels are affected similarly by temperature-induced
fluorescence quenching.

HOWEVER: The compensation may not be perfect because:
- The 405nm and 465nm excitation wavelengths probe different chromophore states
  (protonated vs deprotonated) which may have different temperature coefficients
- Temperature effects on quantum yield may differ between excitation wavelengths
- The calcium-dependent component at 465nm adds complexity

4c. Does z-scoring help or hurt with the temperature confound?
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Z-SCORING DOES NOT ADDRESS THE TEMPERATURE CONFOUND. Here's why:

If you z-score relative to a pre-heating baseline:
- The mean and std are computed at baseline temperature (e.g., 37C)
- During heating, the raw fluorescence changes for TWO reasons:
  (1) real calcium/neural activity changes, and (2) thermal quenching artifact
- The z-score will reflect BOTH of these effects mixed together
- You cannot separate them post-hoc by z-scoring alone

Z-scoring normalizes for BETWEEN-ANIMAL differences in SNR and expression level,
but it does NOT correct for WITHIN-RECORDING artifacts that correlate with the
experimental manipulation (i.e., temperature).

4d. Recommendations for addressing the temperature confound
---------------------------------------------------------------------
(1) ISOSBESTIC CORRECTION IS ESSENTIAL: The 405nm channel should partially
    capture the thermal quenching artifact. After proper isosbestic subtraction,
    the residual dF/F should be enriched for calcium-dependent signal. However,
    verify this by examining whether the isosbestic channel shows a clear
    temperature-correlated drift during heating.

(2) CONTROL EXPERIMENTS: The gold standard is to include animals expressing a
    calcium-INSENSITIVE fluorescent protein (e.g., eGFP alone, not GCaMP) under
    the same heating protocol. Any fluorescence changes in eGFP-only animals are
    purely artifactual and provide the expected magnitude of the thermal artifact.

(3) IN VITRO CALIBRATION: Measure GCaMP fluorescence at different temperatures
    in a cuvette or on a heated microscope stage to quantify the expected
    fluorescence change per degree C for your specific sensor variant.

(4) HEMODYNAMIC CONFOUNDS: Zhang et al. (2021, bioRxiv) demonstrated that
    hemoglobin absorption changes can create artifacts in fiber photometry.
    During hyperthermia, vasodilation increases blood volume near the fiber tip,
    which increases hemoglobin absorption and DECREASES measured fluorescence --
    this is an ADDITIONAL confound beyond the thermal quenching.

(5) TEMPERATURE REGRESSION: If you have concurrent brain temperature measurements,
    you could regress out the temperature effect from the fluorescence signal,
    analogous to how the isosbestic channel is used for motion correction.

5. SHOULD THE BASELINE EXCLUDE THE FIRST FEW MINUTES? (PHOTOBLEACHING TRANSIENT)
================================================================================

SHORT ANSWER: YES. Strongly recommended to exclude the first 2-5 minutes.

5a. The initial photobleaching transient
---------------------------------------------------------------------
When excitation light is first turned on, there is a rapid exponential decay in
fluorescence that is NOT related to neural activity. This is photobleaching --
the irreversible destruction of fluorophore molecules by excitation light.

Key characteristics:
- The initial decay is fastest in the first 1-3 minutes
- It follows an exponential (or bi-exponential) decay curve
- The rate depends on excitation power, sensor expression level, and fiber NA
- After the initial transient, bleaching continues but at a much slower rate
  that can be modeled and removed

5b. Evidence and recommendations from the literature
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Simpson et al. (2024, Neuron -- "Lights, fiber, action!"):
- Explicitly discusses photobleaching as a major artifact
- Recommends allowing time for photobleaching to stabilize before recording data
- Their Figure 3 shows the preprocessing pipeline with photobleaching correction

Keevers & Jean-Richard-dit-Bressel (2025, Neurophotonics):
- "Photobleaching occurs when a fluorophore is exposed to excitation light,
  causing it to output less fluorescence. Given photometry involves exposing the
  biosensor to excitation light across a recording session, this results in
  signal decay across the session."
- They recommend low-pass filtering + robust regression for addressing bleaching

Neurophotometrics (commercial vendor documentation):
- Recommends turning on LEDs 5-10 minutes before the experiment starts
- The initial rapid photobleaching can confound baseline measurements

FPmotion (Hong et al., 2026, bioRxiv):
- Their pipeline performs detrending specifically to address photobleaching

5c. Practical recommendation for Chandni's baseline
---------------------------------------------------------------------
RECOMMENDED APPROACH:
1. Turn on LEDs and begin recording
2. EXCLUDE the first 2-5 minutes from the baseline period
3. Use the remaining pre-heating period (after photobleaching stabilizes, before
   heating onset) as the baseline for z-scoring
4. Alternatively, fit an exponential decay to the initial signal and subtract it
   before computing any normalization

If the recording starts at t=0 and heating begins at t=T:
- BAD baseline:  t=0 to t=T  (includes photobleaching transient)
- GOOD baseline: t=3min to t=T (or t=5min to t=T)

The exact cutoff depends on the rate of photobleaching in your specific setup.
Plot the raw 405nm and 465nm signals and visually identify when the rapid initial
decay stabilizes into a slower, approximately linear drift.

6. CROSS-ANIMAL NORMALIZATION: IS Z-SCORING SUFFICIENT?
================================================================================

SHORT ANSWER: Z-scoring is the standard first step but may not be sufficient
depending on the analysis.

6a. What z-scoring does for cross-animal comparison
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Z-scoring addresses:
- Different absolute fluorescence levels (fiber placement, expression level)
- Different noise floors (fiber quality, LED power, detector sensitivity)
- Different baseline activity levels

By expressing everything in units of "baseline standard deviations," animals
with different absolute signal magnitudes can be compared on the same scale.

6b. Limitations of z-scoring for cross-animal comparison
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Z-scoring does NOT address:
- Differences in sensor expression DENSITY (more GCaMP = larger dF/F for same
  calcium change, but also potentially higher baseline variance)
- Differences in DYNAMIC RANGE of the sensor (saturation effects)
- Differences in the RELATIONSHIP between fluorescence and the underlying
  biology (i.e., whether a 1% dF/F means the same thing in two animals)
- Non-linear sensor responses at different expression levels

6c. Additional steps that may be needed
---------------------------------------------------------------------
(1) VERIFY COMPARABLE BASELINE CHARACTERISTICS:
    Before comparing z-scored data across animals, confirm that baseline
    statistics (SNR, transient frequency, raw dF/F magnitude) are in a
    comparable range. Exclude animals with very poor SNR or no detectable signal.

(2) FOR TRANSIENT PROPERTIES (using raw dF/F):
    If comparing transient amplitudes across animals in raw dF/F, you should:
    - Ensure comparable sensor expression (histology)
    - Ensure comparable fiber placement (histology)
    - Consider normalizing amplitudes to each animal's own baseline transient
      amplitudes (i.e., fold-change relative to pre-manipulation transients)

(3) MIXED EFFECTS MODELS:
    Loewinger et al. (2025, eLife) propose functional linear mixed models that
    account for between-animal variability at every time point, rather than
    reducing to summary statistics. This is a more principled approach than
    simple z-scoring + averaging.

(4) BOOTSTRAP CONFIDENCE INTERVALS:
    Drakopoulos et al. (2025, eNeuro -- FiPhoPHA) implement bootstrapped
    confidence intervals for group comparisons that do not require z-scoring.

7. RECOMMENDATIONS FROM MAJOR FIBER PHOTOMETRY ANALYSIS PAPERS
================================================================================

7a. Simpson, Akam, Patriarchi et al. (2024) -- "Lights, fiber, action!"
    Neuron, 112(5), 718-739
---------------------------------------------------------------------
THE most comprehensive recent primer. Key recommendations:
- Preprocessing: (1) Low-pass filter, (2) Isosbestic correction via regression,
  (3) dF/F calculation, (4) optional z-scoring for group comparisons
- WARNS about the lack of consensus on analysis methods
- Emphasizes that the isosbestic channel is critical for artifact correction
- Notes that z-scoring is commonly used but that the choice of baseline and
  normalization strategy should be justified for each experiment
- Discusses the importance of control experiments (e.g., eGFP-only controls)
- Cautions that photometry signals can be confounded by hemodynamic changes
- Recommends reporting both raw and normalized data

7b. Sherathiya, Schaid, Seiler, Lopez & Lerner (2021) -- GuPPy
    Scientific Reports, 11, 24212
---------------------------------------------------------------------
From the Lerner lab (Talia Lerner was a pioneer of fiber photometry). GuPPy
implements:
- 405nm/465nm isosbestic correction via linear regression
- dF/F computation: dF/F = (F_465 - F_fitted_405) / F_fitted_405
- Z-scoring for peri-event analysis
- The pipeline is designed for event-locked analysis with trial-level z-scoring
  relative to a pre-event baseline window
- Does not specifically address session-wide baseline z-scoring

7c. Martianova, Aronson & Proulx (2019) -- Multi-Fiber Photometry protocol
    Journal of Visualized Experiments, 152, e60278
    + GitHub: katemartian/Photometry_data_processing
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Key contributions:
- Introduced the "zdFF" computation using airPLS baseline estimation
- Pipeline: smooth signals -> baseline correction with airPLS -> standardize ->
  compute zdFF
- Their approach effectively uses the 405nm channel for motion/bleaching
  correction and produces a z-scored dF/F output
- Widely cited and used as a starting template for many labs

7d. Lerner et al. (2015) -- original fiber photometry in striatum
    Cell, 162(3), 635-647
---------------------------------------------------------------------
One of the foundational fiber photometry papers. Established:
- Simultaneous recording from direct and indirect pathway neurons
- Used 405nm isosbestic correction
- Computed dF/F relative to fitted isosbestic
- Z-scoring for cross-animal and cross-session comparisons
- Set the standard that most subsequent papers followed

7e. Keevers & Jean-Richard-dit-Bressel (2025) -- Artifact correction
    Neurophotonics, 12(2), 025003
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Most rigorous recent treatment of preprocessing. Key findings:
- OLS regression for isosbestic fitting is BIASED -- it "chases" real neural
  signals in the 465nm channel, partially subtracting them
- IRLS (robust) regression is superior -- downweights outlier points that
  correspond to real neural events
- Low-pass filtering BEFORE regression improves the fit
- dF/F is preferable to simple dF (subtraction without normalization)
- Provides specific recommendations for a preprocessing pipeline:
  1. Low-pass filter both channels
  2. Fit isosbestic to signal via IRLS regression
  3. Compute dF/F = (F_signal - F_fitted_iso) / F_fitted_iso

7f. Wallace et al. (2025) -- "The z-scored data are not the data"
    bioRxiv / ACS Chemical Neuroscience
---------------------------------------------------------------------
[Detailed above in Section 2a]
Critical paper arguing that z-scoring destroys transient kinetic information.
Specifically warns against using z-scored data for:
- Peak amplitude measurements
- Peak width measurements
- Peak frequency measurements (when signal is reduced)
Recommends the "Prominence Method" as a compromise or raw %dF/F analysis.

7g. Loewinger, Cui, Lovinger & Pereira (2025) -- Statistical framework
    eLife, 13, RP95802
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Proposes functional linear mixed models for photometry analysis:
- Avoids the need to condense signals into summary statistics (AUC, peak, etc.)
- Enables hypothesis testing at every time point within a trial
- Accounts for between-animal variability
- Demonstrates improved statistical power over standard approaches
- Does not require z-scoring per se, but works on preprocessed dF/F data

7h. Drakopoulos et al. (2025) -- FiPhoPHA
    eNeuro, 12(8), ENEURO.0221-25.2025
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Post-hoc statistical analysis package:
- Uses bootstrapped confidence intervals instead of parametric tests
- Implements permutation tests for comparing conditions
- Provides unbiased analysis without requiring z-scoring
- Emphasizes that user-defined time bins introduce bias

================================================================================
SYNTHESIS AND RECOMMENDATIONS FOR CHANDNI'S HYPERTHERMIA EXPERIMENT
================================================================================

CHANDNI'S CURRENT APPROACH:
- Z-score dF/F relative to baseline period (recording start to heating onset)
- Use z-dF/F for mean signal analyses
- Use raw dF/F for transient properties (amplitude, half-width)

ASSESSMENT: This approach is fundamentally sound and well-supported by the
literature, with the following important caveats and refinements:

REFINEMENT 1: EXCLUDE INITIAL PHOTOBLEACHING
- Do NOT include the first 2-5 minutes of recording in the baseline
- Use the period from ~3-5 minutes after recording start until heating onset
- Verify by plotting raw fluorescence that the rapid exponential decay has
  stabilized before the baseline window begins

REFINEMENT 2: VERIFY ISOSBESTIC CORRECTION ADEQUACY
- Before z-scoring, ensure the isosbestic (405nm) correction has been applied
- Consider using IRLS regression instead of OLS for the isosbestic fit
  (per Keevers & Jean-Richard-dit-Bressel 2025)
- Check whether the 405nm channel shows a drift during heating -- if so, this
  suggests the isosbestic correction IS capturing some of the thermal artifact

REFINEMENT 3: ADDRESS THE TEMPERATURE CONFOUND
- The GCaMP temperature sensitivity (~1-2% fluorescence decrease per degree C)
  is a MAJOR confound that z-scoring cannot address
- The isosbestic channel should partially compensate, but may not fully correct
- ESSENTIAL CONTROL: Include eGFP-only (non-calcium-sensitive) animals under
  the same heating protocol, OR compare to GCaMP animals with known neural
  silencing during heating
- If no control animals are available, at minimum examine the 405nm channel
  during heating to quantify the magnitude of the thermal artifact
- Consider plotting the 405nm signal vs. temperature to establish the thermal
  coefficient, then verify that this is adequately removed from the dF/F

REFINEMENT 4: USE ROBUST Z-SCORING IF BASELINE IS NOISY
- If the baseline period contains transients or motion artifacts, consider
  using median/MAD-based robust z-scoring instead of mean/std
- This prevents a few large events from inflating the denominator

REFINEMENT 5: FOR TRANSIENT ANALYSIS ON RAW dF/F
- Chandni's approach of using raw dF/F for amplitude and half-width is
  CORRECT per Wallace et al. (2025)
- For cross-animal comparison of transient properties, consider normalizing
  each animal's transients to its own baseline transient statistics (e.g.,
  fold-change in amplitude relative to pre-heating transients)

REFINEMENT 6: CROSS-ANIMAL COMPARISON
- Z-scoring is sufficient for mean signal comparisons across animals
- For transient properties, additional normalization (within-animal fold-change)
  or mixed-effects modeling (Loewinger et al. 2025) may be needed
- Always verify histology to confirm comparable fiber placement and expression
